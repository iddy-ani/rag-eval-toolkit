Understanding and Detecting Hallucination in Large Language Models

Hallucination in the context of large language models refers to the generation of text that is fluent and plausible-sounding but factually incorrect, fabricated, or not grounded in the provided context. This phenomenon is one of the most significant challenges in deploying LLMs in production environments, particularly in applications where accuracy and trustworthiness are critical.

There are two primary types of hallucination. Intrinsic hallucination occurs when the model generates information that contradicts its source material or provided context. For example, if a RAG system retrieves a document stating that a company was founded in 2015, but the model generates a response claiming the company was founded in 2010, that is intrinsic hallucination. Extrinsic hallucination occurs when the model generates information that cannot be verified from any provided source, essentially making up facts that appear reasonable but have no basis in the available evidence.

Several factors contribute to hallucination in LLMs. The training process itself encourages fluent generation, which can lead models to produce confident-sounding text even when they lack reliable information. The attention mechanism in transformer models can sometimes focus on the wrong parts of the input, leading to misinterpretation of context. Additionally, when the model encounters a gap in its knowledge or retrieved context, it may fill that gap with plausible-sounding but fabricated information rather than acknowledging uncertainty.

Detecting hallucination requires systematic evaluation approaches. One common method is LLM-as-judge evaluation, where a separate language model is used to assess whether the generated output is faithful to the provided context. The evaluator model is given the context documents and the generated response, then asked to determine if every claim in the response is supported by the context. This approach scales better than human evaluation while maintaining reasonable accuracy.

Faithfulness scoring quantifies how well a response is grounded in the retrieved evidence. The response is decomposed into individual claims, and each claim is verified against the source documents. The faithfulness score is the ratio of supported claims to total claims. A score of 1.0 means every claim is supported, while a lower score indicates potential hallucination.

Production systems should implement hallucination detection as a quality gate rather than an afterthought. Responses that score below a confidence threshold should be flagged for human review or replaced with a fallback response that acknowledges the limitation. Statistical analysis using confidence intervals can help determine whether changes to the system actually reduce hallucination rates or if observed differences are due to random variation.

Reducing hallucination in RAG systems involves improving both retrieval quality and generation quality. Better retrieval means providing the model with more relevant and accurate context, leaving fewer gaps to fill with fabricated information. On the generation side, techniques like constrained decoding, chain-of-thought prompting, and explicit grounding instructions in system prompts can help the model stay closer to the provided evidence.
