Vector Databases and Embeddings: The Foundation of Modern Retrieval Systems

At the core of any modern retrieval system lies the concept of embeddings. An embedding is a dense numerical vector that represents the semantic meaning of a piece of text. Unlike traditional keyword representations where each word maps to a single dimension, embeddings compress meaning into a fixed-size vector, typically between 384 and 1536 dimensions, where similar concepts end up close together in the vector space.

Embedding models are neural networks trained on large text corpora to produce these dense representations. Popular embedding models include OpenAI's text-embedding-ada-002, sentence-transformers models like all-MiniLM-L6-v2, and Cohere's embed models. Each model produces vectors of different dimensions and has different performance characteristics across domains. Smaller models like all-MiniLM-L6-v2 are fast and work well for general-purpose retrieval, while larger models may capture more nuanced semantic relationships.

Vector databases are specialized storage systems optimised for storing and searching through large collections of embedding vectors. Unlike traditional relational databases that match exact values, vector databases perform approximate nearest neighbor search, finding vectors that are closest to a query vector in high-dimensional space. Popular vector databases include Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, ChromaDB, and pgvector, which extends PostgreSQL with vector search capabilities.

The similarity between vectors is typically measured using cosine similarity, which calculates the cosine of the angle between two vectors. A cosine similarity of 1.0 means the vectors point in the same direction and are considered semantically identical, while a value of 0.0 means they are orthogonal and unrelated. Some systems use dot product or Euclidean distance as alternative similarity metrics, each with different properties for different use cases.

Indexing strategies play a crucial role in vector database performance. For small collections of a few thousand vectors, brute-force comparison works well enough. But for millions or billions of vectors, approximate nearest neighbor algorithms like HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World), IVF (Inverted File Index), or PQ (Product Quantisation) are essential. These algorithms trade a small amount of accuracy for dramatic improvements in search speed, reducing search time from linear to sub-linear with respect to the number of vectors.

The choice of vector database and indexing strategy depends on the specific requirements of the application, including the size of the collection, required latency, accuracy requirements, and whether the data needs to be updated frequently. For production RAG systems, the vector database is just one component of a larger retrieval pipeline that may also include metadata filtering, hybrid search with keyword indices, and post-retrieval re-ranking.
